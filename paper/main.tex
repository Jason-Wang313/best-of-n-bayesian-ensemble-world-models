\begin{abstract}
Bayesian and ensemble world models expose epistemic uncertainty, but
uncertainty can be consumed in ways that make planning less safe. This paper
studies \emph{posterior-tail selection}: candidate trajectories are scored by
posterior-sampled world-model values, the highest sampled score is executed,
and the selected trajectory becomes an upper-tail residual of epistemic model
error. In the controlled latent benchmark, sampled-posterior selection at
$N=\PaperHighN{}$ reaches regret \SampleHighRegret{} to the in-set candidate
oracle, while calibrated lower-confidence selection reduces regret to
\PessHighRegret{} and tail selection to \PessHighTail{}. The expanded evidence
adds \VThreeStressConditions{} posterior stress conditions, paired high-$N$
confidence intervals, calibration coverage diagnostics, a learned bootstrap
shift suite, a standard Gymnasium classic-control bridge, and a MuJoCo
continuous-control bridge \citep{towers2024gymnasium,todorov2012mujoco}. In
the nominal stress condition, sampled posterior selection is worse than
calibrated LCB by \VThreeNominalSampleLCBGap{} true-return points with 95\% CI
[\VThreeNominalSampleLCBCILow{}, \VThreeNominalSampleLCBCIHigh{}]. On
\VFourMujocoEnvironments{} Gymnasium MuJoCo environments with
\VFourMujocoProblems{} held-out planning problems, a calibration-gated selector
achieves mean true return \VFourMujocoGateReturn{} versus
\VFourMujocoSampleReturn{} for posterior sampling and reduces in-pool oracle
regret from \VFourMujocoSampleRegret{} to \VFourMujocoGateRegret{}. The claim
is deliberately scoped: this is a CPU-bounded diagnostic and repair study for
posterior aggregation in world-model rollout selection, not real-robot
validation or a MuJoCo/D4RL state-of-the-art claim.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

World models let agents evaluate futures before acting. Modern systems learn
latent dynamics for planning and policy learning \citep{hafner2019planet,
hafner2020dreamer,hafner2023dreamerv3}, use ensembles to represent model
uncertainty \citep{chua2018pets,lakshminarayanan2017deep}, and combine learned
models with model-predictive control or offline policy optimization
\citep{janner2019mbpo,yu2020mopo,kidambi2020morel,yu2021combo}. A common
deployment instinct is to spend more inference-time compute: sample many
candidate action sequences, roll them out in a world model, and choose the
candidate with the highest model score.

That instinct is not automatically safe when the score is a posterior sample.
Posterior sampling is useful when one sampled model is followed coherently as
an exploration hypothesis. A per-candidate posterior-sampled maximum is a
different operator: it searches across candidates for a high posterior draw.
The selected action sequence may therefore be the one for which an uncertain
model member is most optimistic, not the one with high posterior mean or high
true return.

We call this failure \emph{posterior-tail selection}. It is related to
inference-time reward overoptimization in language models
\citep{gao2023scaling,jinnai2024regularized,huang2025bon,yu2026caution,
hsu2026tails}, but the error source and diagnostics differ. In Bayesian world
models, the score comes from imagined dynamics. The appropriate audit measures
selected posterior residuals, ensemble dispersion, OOD mass, hazard-tail
selection, regret to the candidate oracle, and calibration behavior.

The experimental package is deliberately broader than a single deterministic
curve: it includes a posterior stress grid, a learned bootstrap
return-ensemble suite, paired effect-size confidence intervals, calibration
coverage diagnostics, recognized Gymnasium classic-control environments, and
Gymnasium MuJoCo continuous-control environments. These additions do not make
the paper a hardware result or a benchmark leaderboard paper. They make the
mechanism harder to dismiss as a single cherry-picked synthetic setting.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
We make five scoped contributions.
\begin{itemize}
    \item We define posterior-tail selection as a deployment failure mode for
    Bayesian and ensemble world-model planning.
    \item We give a compact selected-residual proposition explaining why a
    maximum over posterior-sampled scores exposes upper-tail model error.
    \item We implement deterministic latent, learned-bootstrap, Gymnasium
    classic-control, and Gymnasium MuJoCo benchmark bridges with
    posterior-sampled, Thompson-style, optimistic, pessimistic, quantile,
    calibration-gated, random, CEM, and oracle selectors.
    \item We add \VThreeStressConditions{} posterior stress conditions,
    \VFourGymProblems{} classic-control planning problems, and
    \VFourMujocoProblems{} MuJoCo planning problems, with paired high-$N$
    confidence intervals, calibration coverage artifacts, and frozen protocol
    JSON files.
    \item We show that calibrated lower-confidence and validation-gated
    selectors sharply reduce selected-tail failure in controlled stress tests
    and improve average return on the MuJoCo bridge, while remaining honestly
    limited on easy classic-control settings and learned-model shift.
\end{itemize}

\section{Mechanism}

Let $a_1,\ldots,a_N$ be candidate action sequences. A Bayesian or ensemble
world model gives each candidate a posterior distribution over imagined
returns. Write a posterior sampled score as
\begin{equation}
    S_i = \mu_i + \epsilon_i,
\end{equation}
where $\mu_i$ is the posterior mean and $\epsilon_i$ is the sampled
model-member residual. The sampled-posterior selector chooses
$\hat{i}=\arg\max_i S_i$.

\paragraph{Diagnostic proposition.}
Suppose $\epsilon_i$ are independent centered residuals conditional on the
candidate set, and at least two candidates have nonzero residual variance.
Then
\begin{equation}
    \mathbb{E}\left[\epsilon_{\hat{i}}\right] \geq 0,
\end{equation}
with strict inequality whenever residual variance can affect the argmax. In
the equal-mean Gaussian residual case with variance $\sigma^2$, the expected
selected residual is $\sigma \mathbb{E}[\max_i Z_i]$, growing like
$\sigma\sqrt{2\log N}$.

The proposition is intentionally modest. It does not say every Bayesian
planner fails or that posterior sampling is wrong. It says that the maximum
operator changes what posterior samples mean: increasing the candidate budget
selects not only high estimated value, but also high residual optimism.

\paragraph{Repair principle.}
The repair tested here is a lower-confidence score
\begin{equation}
    L_i = \mu_i - \beta \sigma_i,
\end{equation}
where $\sigma_i$ is ensemble standard deviation and $\beta$ is fitted on held
out calibration candidates as a quantile of
$|\mu_i - V_i|/(\sigma_i+\epsilon)$. The rule is deliberately simple. If a
scalar calibrated margin removes most of the selected-tail failure, the
failure is visible and auditable rather than mysterious.

\paragraph{Validation-gated selector.}
The real-environment bridge also uses a small pre-test selector gate. On
held-out calibration starts, we evaluate deployable selectors
\{posterior mean, posterior sample, pessimistic, quantile, calibrated LCB\}
against true environment returns and freeze the best selector family before
test measurement. Near-ties are resolved toward calibrated LCB, then quantile,
then pessimistic, then posterior-sampled, then mean selection. This rule is not
allowed to see test starts or test returns. Its role is to prevent a paper-thin
claim that one scalar pessimism level is universally optimal; if calibration
tasks favor sample-based selection, the gate can choose it, and if calibration
is ambiguous, the gate chooses the safer lower-tail rule.

\section{Experimental Design}

\subsection{Base latent dynamics benchmark}

We build a two-dimensional latent control system with position and velocity.
The true dynamics reward approaching a goal region and penalize entering a
high-position hazard. Candidate action sequences are smooth random controls
with occasional high-excursion probes. The posterior ensemble is accurate near
the training-like region but uncertain about hazard strength and terminal
value in high-position regions.

For every problem instance, we draw a shared candidate set of size
$\PaperHighN{}$ and evaluate smaller budgets by prefixing the same set. This
makes curves across $N$ comparable. The full sweep uses \PaperSeeds{} posterior
seeds and \PaperProblems{} candidate problems. Detailed rows are written to
\texttt{results/full/results.csv}; paper figures are generated from
\texttt{results/full/summary.csv}.

\subsection{Selectors}

The deployed selectors are intentionally diverse:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Posterior mean}: choose the candidate with largest ensemble
    mean.
    \item \textbf{Sampled posterior}: draw one model member score per
    candidate and choose the largest sampled score.
    \item \textbf{Thompson member}: draw one model member and rank all
    candidates by that member.
    \item \textbf{Mean + standard deviation}: an optimistic upper-confidence
    selector.
    \item \textbf{Pessimistic, quantile, and calibrated LCB}: robust
    posterior-tail repairs.
    \item \textbf{Random and oracle}: lower reference and in-set candidate
    upper reference.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Stress and learned suites}

The expanded evidence package adds two bounded suites. The posterior stress suite evaluates
\VThreeStressConditions{} conditions: nominal, low/high blindspot, low/high
posterior spread, gentle/wide action generators, and two shifted combinations.
Each condition uses \VThreeStressReplicates{} candidate problems at high $N$,
with the same selector set and held-out calibration rule.

The learned suite trains a bootstrap ridge return ensemble on synthetic
rollout returns from a narrower action distribution and evaluates it under a
wider action distribution. This is not a neural world model benchmark, but it
is an important step beyond hand-coded posterior members: model uncertainty
comes from finite-data bootstrap fitting, and selection is evaluated on
held-out shifted candidate problems. The suite uses \VThreeLearnedProblems{}
candidate problems and writes detailed artifacts to the generated results
directory.

\subsection{Recognized environment benchmark bridge}

The v4 artifact adds a real-environment planning bridge using Gymnasium
\citep{towers2024gymnasium}. The classic-control tier uses Pendulum-v1 and
MountainCarContinuous-v0. The continuous-control tier uses the Gymnasium
MuJoCo InvertedPendulum-v5 and Reacher-v5 environments, backed by the MuJoCo
physics engine \citep{todorov2012mujoco}. For each environment and seed, we
collect transition data from the true simulator under a narrower training
action distribution, fit a randomized-prior bootstrap dynamics/reward ensemble
\citep{osband2018randomized}, fit the LCB multiplier and validation gate on
held-out calibration starts, and then freeze the protocol before test starts
are evaluated.

At test time, every selector sees the same candidate action-sequence prefix
for a given start and budget. The candidate pool contains shuffled random,
smoothed, sinusoidal, and generic control sequences, while CEM and scripted
controllers are reported as standalone baselines rather than being inserted
into the selector pool. Final returns are measured by executing the selected
sequence in the true environment from the same saved simulator state. The
candidate oracle is an in-pool upper reference; it is not a deployable method.
The frozen artifacts are
\texttt{results/v4/protocol\_freeze.json} and
\texttt{results/v4\_mujoco/protocol\_freeze.json}. They record seeds,
candidate budgets, environments, baselines, calibration settings, claim gates,
and artifact hashes.

This bridge is intentionally scoped. It removes the synthetic-only rejection
vector and tests the mechanism on recognized environments, but it does not
claim MuJoCo state-of-the-art performance, D4RL performance, real-robot
validation, or large-scale video-world-model dominance.

\section{Related Work}

\paragraph{World models and ensemble planning.}
PlaNet and Dreamer learn latent dynamics for imagination and control
\citep{hafner2019planet,hafner2020dreamer,hafner2023dreamerv3}. PETS uses
probabilistic ensembles with trajectory sampling for sample-efficient
continuous control \citep{chua2018pets}. MBPO studies when short model rollouts
are useful for policy optimization \citep{janner2019mbpo}. These works motivate
the architecture, while our focus is the inference-time aggregation operator
over posterior-sampled candidate scores.

\paragraph{Benchmarks and simulator interfaces.}
Accepted model-based RL papers usually report recognized control domains
rather than only toy synthetic diagnostics: PETS and MBPO use MuJoCo-style
continuous control \citep{chua2018pets,janner2019mbpo,todorov2012mujoco}, and
sequence/offline RL papers often use public benchmark suites for fair
comparison \citep{janner2021trajectory}. Gymnasium standardizes environment
interfaces and is the maintained successor to OpenAI Gym
\citep{towers2024gymnasium}. Our benchmark bridge follows that convention
without pretending to be a MuJoCo leaderboard paper: the recognized
environments test whether the selected-tail mechanism survives outside the
synthetic posterior generator.

\paragraph{Pessimism and model exploitation.}
MOPO, MOReL, COMBO, RAMBO, and CQL use pessimism or conservatism to avoid model
or value overestimation in offline RL \citep{yu2020mopo,kidambi2020morel,
yu2021combo,rigter2022rambo,kumar2020cql}. Our repair is narrower: a
calibrated lower-confidence score for selecting from a candidate set. The
contribution is the selected-tail diagnostic, not a new pessimistic RL
algorithm.

\paragraph{Bayesian uncertainty.}
Deep ensembles, MC dropout, bootstrapped value functions, randomized priors,
and posterior-sampling RL provide uncertainty tools
\citep{lakshminarayanan2017deep,gal2016dropout,osband2016bootstrapped,
osband2018randomized,fan2021posterior}. Recent robotic world-model work also
emphasizes epistemic uncertainty for deployment \citep{li2025rwm}. The current
diagnostic asks how uncertainty is consumed by maximum selection.

\paragraph{Inference-time overoptimization.}
Reward-model overoptimization and inference-time alignment work show that
many-candidate selection can reward-hack imperfect learned scorers
\citep{gao2023scaling,jinnai2024regularized,huang2025bon,yu2026caution,
hsu2026tails}. We import the operator-level warning but specialize it to
Bayesian and ensemble world-model rollouts.

\section{Limitations}

The evidence remains deliberately bounded. The controlled benchmark and stress
grid are synthetic, and the learned suites use bootstrap ridge dynamics/reward
models rather than large neural video or robotics world models. The
Gymnasium/MuJoCo bridge removes the synthetic-only rejection vector, but it is
still a compact CPU-bounded benchmark with finite candidate pools; it is not a
state-of-the-art MuJoCo, D4RL, Meta-World, Atari, or robot result. The CEM
baseline is intentionally included as an adversarial comparison, but because it
optimizes the same imperfect learned model it can perform poorly when model
error is severe. The diagnostic proposition assumes centered residuals and
independence that are approximations in learned dynamics. The
lower-confidence and validation-gated repairs are calibration heuristics, not
formal safety guarantees. These boundaries are reflected in the generated
claim-evidence audit and protocol-freeze files.

\section{Reproducibility and Ethics}

The repository includes deterministic code, tests, generated CSVs, figures,
literature notes, hostile-prior analysis, reviewer attacks, protocol-freeze
JSON files, and a claim-evidence audit. The full base sweep, expanded suite,
classic-control bridge, and MuJoCo bridge run on CPU with bounded candidate
counts and no external model downloads. The benchmark uses synthetic data and
standard simulators, with no human-subject data. The safety implication is
cautionary: increasing inference-time candidate search can amplify model error
when the learned score is optimized too aggressively.

\section{Proof Sketch}

For the diagnostic proposition, condition on the candidate set. If
$\hat{i}=\arg\max_i(\mu_i+\epsilon_i)$, then candidates with larger realized
residuals are more likely to be selected. In the equal-mean case the selected
residual is exactly $\max_i \epsilon_i$, so its expectation is nonnegative and
strictly positive for non-degenerate residuals. Unequal means can reduce but do
not erase the mechanism when residual variance changes the argmax with
positive probability. The proposition is used only as a mechanism sketch; the
empirical artifacts measure selected residuals directly.

\section{Implementation Details}

The base posterior ensemble is generated from perturbed latent dynamics:
gain, damping, hazard center, hazard strength, goal shift, value bias, and
terminal bias vary across members. Candidate sequences are smooth controls
with occasional high-excursion probes. Metrics include selected true return,
posterior mean, posterior standard deviation, sampled score, regret to the
in-set oracle, sample-over-mean residual, OOD mass, hazard mass, action energy,
uncertainty percentile, and hazard-tail selection.

The learned bootstrap suite featurizes each action sequence using action
moments, positive/negative action mass, cumulative-action proxies, velocity
proxies, early/late sums, and time-weighted moments. Bootstrap ridge regressors
are trained on true synthetic rollout returns from a narrower action
distribution and evaluated under a wider distribution. This suite is a
lightweight learned value-ensemble stress test, not a replacement for neural
world-model benchmarks.

\section{Reviewer Attack Surface}

\paragraph{Is this still synthetic?}
Yes. The paper says so, and the claim is not real-robot validation. The
expanded evidence is not meant to hide the limitation; it tests whether the mechanism is
robust to stress settings and a learned bootstrap ensemble.

\paragraph{Is pessimism the real contribution?}
No. The contribution is the posterior-tail diagnostic and evidence that the
failure is measurable. Calibrated LCB is a sanity repair showing that the
failure can be reduced with held-out residual calibration.

\paragraph{Does posterior mean already solve the base benchmark?}
Mostly yes, and the paper foregrounds that boundary. The risk is for
deployments that use posterior-sampled, Thompson-like, optimistic, or otherwise
upper-tail scores as the selection objective.

\paragraph{Does the learned suite prove generalization to large world models?}
No. It is a stronger controlled artifact, not a large-scale claim. It uses
finite-data bootstrap uncertainty and evaluation shift to test whether the same
selector pathology appears without hand-coded posterior members.

\section{Compute Budget}

The expanded suite uses \VThreeStressConditions{} stress conditions, three seeds,
bounded candidate pools, 24-member ensembles, and no model downloads. It is
designed to run on CPU in roughly a minute on the development machine. This is
why the evidence is broad across controlled conditions rather than heavy in a
single large environment.

\section{Selector Definitions}

For a candidate set with posterior member predictions
$P_{ij}$ for candidate $i$ and member $j$, the experiment evaluates the
following deterministic or randomized scores:
\begin{align}
    \text{mean}(i) &= \frac{1}{M}\sum_j P_{ij},\\
    \text{std}(i) &= \sqrt{\frac{1}{M}\sum_j (P_{ij}-\text{mean}(i))^2},\\
    \text{sample}(i) &= P_{i,J_i},\quad J_i\sim \text{Uniform}\{1,\ldots,M\},\\
    \text{Thompson}(i) &= P_{i,J},\quad J\sim \text{Uniform}\{1,\ldots,M\},\\
    \text{UCB}(i) &= \text{mean}(i)+\text{std}(i),\\
    \text{LCB}(i) &= \text{mean}(i)-\beta\,\text{std}(i).
\end{align}
The posterior-sampled selector uses an independently sampled member index per
candidate. The Thompson-member selector uses one sampled member for the whole
candidate set. This separation matters: coherent Thompson-style ranking tests a
single posterior hypothesis, while per-candidate posterior sampling searches
across posterior outliers.

The calibrated LCB uses a held-out calibration set. Let $V_i$ be true synthetic
return on calibration candidates. The scalar
\begin{equation}
    \beta = Q_{0.85}\left(\frac{|\text{mean}(i)-V_i|}{\text{std}(i)+10^{-8}}\right)
\end{equation}
is clipped to a bounded interval and then reused on test candidate problems.
Test true returns are not used by the selector.

\section{Calibration Coverage Diagnostics}

The calibration coverage file records, for each suite and condition, the rate
at which the selected calibrated-LCB trajectory still has positive optimism
gap. This is not a formal conformal guarantee; it is a falsification-oriented
diagnostic. If calibrated LCB often selected trajectories whose posterior mean
remained above true return, the repair would be unreliable even in the
controlled setting. In the posterior stress suite, optimistic-miss rates remain
small in the listed conditions, while the learned bootstrap suite is more
fragile and exposes the cost of finite-data return modeling under evaluation
shift.

This distinction is important for reviewer honesty. The artifact does not
claim that calibration solves learned world-model shift. It shows that:
\begin{itemize}
    \item posterior-sampled selection is highly fragile in the designed
    posterior stress conditions;
    \item calibrated LCB is consistently stronger there;
    \item under learned bootstrap shift, all non-oracle deployed selectors are
    weak, but LCB is still stronger than sampled-posterior selection on paired
    high-$N$ problems.
\end{itemize}

\section{Learned Bootstrap Protocol}

The learned suite uses synthetic data but removes hand-coded posterior members.
For each seed, it samples training action sequences from a narrower action
distribution and evaluates true returns under the latent dynamics. It featurizes
each action sequence using action moments, cumulative-action proxies,
velocity-proxy summaries, high-excursion masses, and time-weighted moments.
Then it fits a bootstrap ridge ensemble. At evaluation time, candidate actions
come from a wider distribution. The same selector set is applied to the
candidate-by-member prediction matrix.

This protocol is intentionally modest and reproducible. It does not require
PyTorch, a GPU, a simulator license, or external data. The cost is that it is
not a full neural world model. The benefit is that it creates a learned
finite-data posterior-like ensemble whose uncertainty comes from bootstrap
resampling rather than from hand-picked model-member parameters.

\section{Real Environment Protocol Freeze}

The real-environment bridge is frozen before final reporting. The classic
control protocol uses \VFourGymEnvironments{} Gymnasium environments,
\VFourGymEnsembleSize{} bootstrap ensemble members, candidate budgets up to
\VFourGymHighN{}, and \VFourGymProblems{} held-out planning problems. The
MuJoCo protocol uses \VFourMujocoEnvironments{} Gymnasium MuJoCo environments,
\VFourMujocoEnsembleSize{} bootstrap ensemble members, candidate budgets up to
\VFourMujocoHighN{}, and \VFourMujocoProblems{} held-out planning problems.
The files \texttt{protocol\_freeze.json} in each v4 result directory record
the final environment names, seeds, training transition counts, calibration
problem counts, candidate budgets, selector list, claim gates, and artifact
hashes.

For both bridges, training transitions are collected from the real simulator
under a narrower action distribution than the test candidate generator. This
creates a mild but deliberate support-shift condition without requiring large
offline datasets. Bootstrap models predict next-state deltas and rewards.
Calibration starts are held out from training data; they fit the LCB multiplier
and the validation-gate family. Test starts are then evaluated once under the
frozen protocol. The final CSVs therefore separate development feedback from
reported measurement: development smoke runs live in separate directories and
are not used by paper macros.

\section{Candidate Pools and Standalone Baselines}

Candidate pools are shared across selectors for each test start. In the
classic-control bridge, pools contain shuffled smooth random sequences and
generic controls. In the MuJoCo bridge, pools contain shuffled smoothed-random,
sinusoidal, and generic control sequences. Prefix budgets create a monotone
candidate-budget audit: $N=8$ is a strict prefix of the $N=32$ and
$N=96$ pools. The candidate oracle is computed by executing every candidate in
the same prefix in the true environment, making regret a within-start,
within-pool quantity.

Strong baselines are not slipped into the selector pool. CEM is run as a
standalone model-based baseline over the same learned ensemble. In classic
control, a scripted teacher is also reported as a standalone reference. This
separation is important: if a strong scripted or CEM sequence is always placed
among the first few candidates, every selector can appear strong for the wrong
reason. The v4 implementation intentionally avoids that confound.

\section{MuJoCo Failure Analysis}

The MuJoCo bridge is the most adversarial v4 evidence. Across the two MuJoCo
environments, the sampled-minus-LCB paired gap is
\VFourMujocoSampleLCBGap{}; equivalently, the validation-gated/LCB rule is
\VFourMujocoGateSampleGap{} true-return points above posterior sampling on
average. The per-environment paired table shows that lower-tail selection
avoids some early termination failures. On Reacher-v5, posterior mean is competitive
because the learned model often ranks a low-regret candidate near the oracle,
but UCB performs badly: the paired Reacher UCB-minus-LCB gap in
\texttt{mujoco\_paired\_effects.csv} is strongly negative, showing that
upper-confidence search can chase model tails even in a recognized simulator.

The validation gate chooses calibrated LCB in the final MuJoCo run because
calibration either favors it outright or leaves the selectors tied within the
near-tie margin. This is a useful result rather than a hidden weakness. It says
that, under the frozen finite-data model and candidate generator, calibration
does not justify upper-tail search. The gate then reduces average regret from
\VFourMujocoSampleRegret{} for posterior sampling to
\VFourMujocoGateRegret{} while staying transparent about the wide confidence
interval [\VFourMujocoGateSampleCILow{}, \VFourMujocoGateSampleCIHigh{}].

The paper still should not be sold as a large-scale world-model benchmark. It

\subsection{Negative Controls and Null Findings}

\paragraph{Classic-control near ties.}
The classic-control bridge is a null separation result. It still belongs in
the paper because it is a recognized benchmark and it checks for gross
implementation failure. If the calibrated selector were worse than random or
failed to match simple low-energy candidates, the method would be suspect.
Instead, the result says the benchmark is too easy or too dominated by simple
candidate prefixes to adjudicate posterior-tail selection.

\paragraph{CEM over model score.}
CEM is not a guaranteed strong baseline when the learned model is wrong. In
the MuJoCo bridge, CEM optimizes the same imperfect bootstrap ensemble and can
therefore amplify model error. This is not hidden; the main table reports CEM
return \VFourMujocoCEMReturn{}. The lesson is aligned with the paper's
mechanism: better optimization over a flawed learned score can make the chosen
sequence worse.

\paragraph{Candidate oracle gap.}
In both controlled and MuJoCo suites, the in-pool oracle is often much stronger
than deployed selectors. That gap is useful because it separates two
hypotheses. If the candidate oracle were weak, failure would be a generation
problem. When the oracle is strong and deployed selectors still have regret,
the failure is a ranking and uncertainty-consumption problem.

\paragraph{No SOTA claim.}
The bridge uses recognized environments but not full leaderboard protocols,
policy learning, replay buffers, or neural world models. The paper therefore
does not claim to beat PETS, Dreamer, MBPO, or offline RL algorithms. Its claim
is narrower: when a world-model planner ranks candidate futures using
posterior samples or upper-tail scores, the maximum operator can select model
error, and calibration can reduce that failure.

\subsection{Extended Related-Work Positioning}

\paragraph{Versus PETS-style planning.}
PETS propagates uncertainty through probabilistic dynamics ensembles and uses
model-predictive control to improve sample efficiency. The present paper is not
a replacement for PETS and does not attempt to match its full continuous-control
training protocol. The narrower question is what happens after a planner has a
candidate set and an ensemble of imagined returns. PETS-style planners can use
trajectory sampling, moment matching, or particles; this paper isolates a
different axis: whether the selection operator consumes uncertainty as a
coherent hypothesis, a posterior mean, a lower confidence bound, or a
per-candidate upper-tail sample. That axis is often implicit in model-based RL
implementations, but it can dominate behavior when candidate budgets grow.

\paragraph{Versus Dreamer-style latent imagination.}
Dreamer and related latent world-model agents learn compact latent dynamics and
optimize policies through imagined rollouts. Their main challenge is learning a
latent representation and policy that transfers to the environment. Our
benchmark is deliberately smaller: it does not learn pixels, representation
models, or actor-critic policies. The advantage of the smaller setup is that it
makes the inference-time aggregation bug visible. A large latent agent could
hide the same bug behind representation learning, value bootstrapping, or
policy optimization. The diagnostic proposed here can be viewed as an audit
that should be run inside larger world-model systems, not as a competitor to
them.

\paragraph{Versus offline pessimism.}
Offline RL pessimism penalizes actions whose values are uncertain because the
dataset does not support them. This paper shares the support-awareness instinct
but differs in two important ways. First, the failure occurs at inference time
inside a candidate-set maximum, not only during value-function training.
Second, the evidence measures the selected candidate's posterior residual,
selected uncertainty, OOD mass, and oracle regret directly. This makes the
paper closer to an operator audit than a new offline RL objective. The
calibrated LCB is intentionally simple so that the experiment tests the
mechanism, not an elaborate algorithm.

\paragraph{Versus inference-time alignment and Best-of-N work.}
Language-model Best-of-N work studies reward-model overoptimization when many
outputs are sampled and the highest learned-reward candidate is selected. The
operator is similar, but the error source differs. In alignment, the learned
reward model may be misspecified relative to human preference. In this paper,
the score is an imagined return from a Bayesian or ensemble world model, and
the selected tail comes from epistemic model uncertainty. The paper therefore
uses diagnostics that are natural for world models: ensemble standard
deviation, sampled-member residual, dynamics support shift, and regret to a
candidate oracle measured in the true simulator.

\paragraph{Versus uncertainty calibration papers.}
Calibration work asks whether predictive uncertainty matches empirical error.
This paper uses calibration as an intervention inside a selection operator.
The important distinction is selection bias: a model can have reasonable
average calibration and still fail after the maximum operator filters for
optimistic errors. The calibration files therefore report selected-candidate
behavior, not only average residuals. This is why paired high-$N$ effects and
selected-tail rates are more informative than global calibration curves alone.

\subsection{Detailed Interpretation by Suite}

\paragraph{Base latent dynamics.}
The base suite is the cleanest mechanism demonstration. It creates a hazard
region that has epistemic uncertainty and high apparent value under some
posterior members. As candidate budget grows, posterior-sampled selection gets
more chances to find a candidate whose sampled member is optimistic. The
candidate oracle remains strong, so high regret is not caused by an empty
candidate pool. The selected trajectory diagnostics close the loop: the
sampled-posterior winner has elevated sample-over-mean residual, OOD mass,
posterior standard deviation, and hazard-tail selection rate.

\paragraph{Posterior stress grid.}
The stress grid answers a reviewer who says the base posterior was
hand-designed. Blindspot strength, posterior spread, and action-generator width
are varied separately and jointly. If the failure appeared only in the nominal
setting, the paper would be fragile. Instead, calibrated LCB wins in
\VThreeStressLCBWinConditions{} of \VThreeStressConditions{} conditions. The
stress grid also identifies scope: if posterior spread is too low or candidate
generation never reaches uncertain regions, the selected-tail failure weakens.
That is a desirable diagnostic property rather than a contradiction.

\paragraph{Learned bootstrap shift.}
The learned bootstrap suite removes hand-coded posterior members and introduces
finite-data uncertainty. It is also more humbling. All non-oracle selectors are
far below the candidate oracle, which means the learned return ensemble is not
good enough to rank the best shifted candidates. This is a stronger and more
honest result than a perfect-looking synthetic plot. It says calibrated LCB is
comparatively better, but learned-model shift remains difficult. The paper
therefore avoids claiming that scalar pessimism solves model-based RL.

\paragraph{Classic-control bridge.}
The classic-control bridge is a standard-environment sanity check. It verifies
that the benchmark machinery can collect real simulator transitions, fit
bootstrap dynamics/reward ensembles, freeze calibration, select candidate
plans, execute them in the true environment, and write artifact hashes. Its
scientific result is mostly negative: the selected-tail mechanism is not
strongly separated under the final classic-control candidate pools. That null
finding is included because it protects against cherry-picking. A strong paper
should disclose where the mechanism is visible and where it is not.

\paragraph{MuJoCo bridge.}
The MuJoCo bridge is the strongest recognized-environment evidence. It is still
compact and CPU-bounded, but it has higher-dimensional observations and
continuous-control dynamics. The main lesson is not that the method reaches the
oracle; it does not. The main lesson is comparative: calibrated/gated
selection improves average return over posterior-sampled selection and avoids
the catastrophic upper-confidence failures seen on Reacher-v5. The bridge
therefore supports the mechanism under realistic simulator dynamics while
staying inside the paper's honest scope.

\subsection{Ablation Logic}

\paragraph{Candidate budget.}
Candidate budget is the central ablation because the selected-tail mechanism is
a maximum-over-candidates effect. A one-candidate planner cannot exploit a
tail through selection; a many-candidate planner can. The experiments therefore
report multiple $N$ values and use prefix candidate pools so that differences
across $N$ cannot be attributed to unrelated random draws.

\paragraph{Posterior sample versus Thompson member.}
Per-candidate posterior sampling and coherent Thompson-member ranking are not
the same algorithm. Per-candidate sampling draws a separate member for each
candidate, which creates many chances to find an optimistic residual. Thompson
member ranking draws one posterior hypothesis and ranks all candidates under
that hypothesis. The selector definitions keep these cases separate because a
reviewer could otherwise object that the paper criticizes posterior sampling
too broadly. The paper's critique is specifically about incoherent
per-candidate upper-tail search.

\paragraph{Mean, UCB, LCB, and quantile.}
Posterior mean asks whether uncertainty should simply be averaged out. UCB
asks whether optimism is useful. LCB and quantile selectors ask whether
lower-tail aggregation can avoid model exploitation. The result is not that
mean is always bad or LCB is always optimal. The result is conditional: when
the candidate pool contains uncertain high-tail artifacts, upper-tail selectors
can fail sharply, and calibrated lower-tail scoring reduces that risk.

\paragraph{Calibration gate.}
The validation gate is an ablation over selector families. It answers the
development-time observation that fixed LCB can be too conservative in some
reduced smoke settings. Instead of tuning the final test set, the gate uses
held-out calibration starts to choose the selector family before test
measurement. In the final v4 run, calibration does not justify sample-based
selection, so the gate chooses LCB. If a future domain strongly favors
posterior sampling on calibration, the same gate would permit it.

\paragraph{Standalone baselines.}
The separation between selector pools and standalone baselines is an important
ablation. If CEM or scripted teacher sequences are inserted at the front of
every candidate pool, all selectors can inherit strong actions without earning
them. The v4 benchmark explicitly avoids that design. CEM is measured, but it
does not make the candidate-pool selector problem disappear.

\subsection{What Would Falsify the Claim}

Several outcomes would weaken or falsify the paper's central claim:
\begin{itemize}
    \item posterior-sampled selection never showed elevated selected residuals
    or selected uncertainty as $N$ increased;
    \item high regret occurred only when the candidate oracle was also weak;
    \item LCB reduced predicted score but not true-environment regret;
    \item calibration selected lower-tail rules on calibration but upper-tail
    rules consistently won on held-out test starts;
    \item UCB or posterior-sampled selectors dominated across controlled,
    learned, classic-control, and MuJoCo suites;
    \item stress-grid changes eliminated the effect in all but one
    hand-designed condition.
\end{itemize}

The final artifact does not show these falsifiers. It does show scope
limitations: classic-control separations are small, learned-model shift remains
hard for every selector, and MuJoCo confidence intervals are wide. Those
limitations make the claim narrower but not empty.

\subsection{Additional Implementation Details}

\paragraph{Feature design for learned v4 models.}
The v4 bridge deliberately uses lightweight ridge bootstrap models rather than
deep networks. For classic control, features include bias, state, action,
quadratic terms, state-action interactions, and angle trigonometric terms for
Pendulum-v1. For MuJoCo, features include bias, observation, action,
quadratics, and observation-action interactions. Targets are next-observation
deltas and rewards. MuJoCo features and targets are standardized before ridge
fitting, and randomized prior functions are added to preserve epistemic
diversity under finite data.

\paragraph{Rollout stability.}
Learned model rollouts can leave the training support and create numerically
invalid features. The MuJoCo bridge clips model-predicted observations to a
finite range during imagined rollouts. This does not affect true-environment
measurement; it only prevents a bad learned model from producing infinities
inside candidate scoring. The protocol JSON records finite rollout clipping as
a claim gate so the numerical safeguard is visible.

\paragraph{Simulator state restoration.}
For MuJoCo environments, test and calibration starts are saved as qpos/qvel
snapshots. Candidate sequences are evaluated by restoring the same snapshot,
executing the sequence, and accumulating true rewards. This is critical for
paired comparisons: every selector is measured from the same physical state,
and differences are due to selected actions, not reset randomness.

\paragraph{Artifact hygiene.}
Development smoke runs are written to separate directories such as
\texttt{results/v4\_smoke*}. Paper macros are copied only from
\texttt{results/v4} and \texttt{results/v4\_mujoco}. This separation lets the
development process use baselines as adversarial teachers without contaminating
final reported measurement.

\subsection{Reviewer-Facing Summary of Scope}

The strongest version of the paper is not "we solve world-model planning." It
is this:
\begin{quote}
When a Bayesian or ensemble world-model planner uses many-candidate search and
ranks candidates by posterior samples or upper-confidence scores, the maximum
operator can select epistemic model error. This failure is measurable through
selected residuals, OOD mass, selected uncertainty, oracle regret, and paired
high-budget effects. A held-out calibrated lower-tail rule, optionally chosen
through a pre-test validation gate, reduces the failure in controlled stress
tests and remains competitive in compact recognized-simulator bridges.
\end{quote}

That claim is useful because many systems increase inference-time compute by
sampling more candidates. The paper warns that more candidate search is not
automatically safer when the scoring model is uncertain. The warning is narrow,
but it is actionable: audit selected uncertainty, compare to lower-tail rules,
freeze calibration before final measurement, include in-pool oracles, and
report null settings where the mechanism does not appear.

\subsection{Practical Recommendations}

The experiments suggest a practical checklist for anyone deploying
ensemble-based world-model planning:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Report selected uncertainty, not only average prediction
    uncertainty. The maximum operator changes which part of the uncertainty
    distribution matters.
    \item Compare posterior-sampled and upper-confidence selection against
    posterior mean and calibrated lower-confidence selection on the same
    candidate pools.
    \item Use an in-pool oracle during evaluation. If the oracle is weak,
    improve candidate generation; if the oracle is strong and the selector has
    high regret, improve ranking or uncertainty aggregation.
    \item Fit calibration and selector gates on held-out starts, then freeze
    the protocol before final measurement.
    \item Keep strong planned baselines separate from candidate pools unless
    the scientific question is explicitly about augmented candidate
    generation.
    \item Treat candidate-budget scaling as an adversarial stress test. If
    performance improves at $N=8$ but degrades at $N=96$, the planner may be
    optimizing model error.
\end{itemize}

These recommendations are intentionally independent of the specific ridge
models used in this artifact. A neural latent world model could run the same
audit by replacing the bootstrap predictor with its learned rollout model and
keeping the candidate-pool, calibration, and oracle-regret protocol.

\subsection{Threats to Validity}

\paragraph{Model class.}
The v4 bridge uses ridge bootstrap models rather than deep networks. This
choice makes the artifact deterministic, CPU-light, and easy to audit, but it
also limits representational power. A stronger neural dynamics model might
reduce regret to the candidate oracle. It would not remove the operator-level
risk by itself: if the model remains uncertain or biased in parts of the
candidate space, upper-tail selection can still amplify that error.

\paragraph{Open-loop candidate sequences.}
The experiments select action sequences rather than learning closed-loop
policies. This isolates the selection operator but under-represents full
model-predictive control systems that replan at every step. Replanning can
reduce some open-loop failures, but it can also repeat the same upper-tail
selection at each step. A natural next benchmark is online MPC with the same
calibration-gated selector family and per-step selected-tail diagnostics.

\paragraph{Finite candidate pools.}
The candidate oracle is only an in-pool oracle. It does not represent the best
possible policy or action sequence. This is a limitation and a strength. It
keeps the evaluation tied to the actual decision faced by the selector: among
the candidates it was offered, did it choose a good one? For global control
quality, future work should pair this diagnostic with stronger candidate
generators and policy-learning baselines.

\paragraph{Simulator coverage.}
Pendulum, MountainCarContinuous, InvertedPendulum, and Reacher are recognized
environments, but they are not enough to claim broad robotics validity. The
paper should be followed by MuJoCo locomotion, DMControl, Meta-World, D4RL, and
pixel-based latent-world-model settings. The contribution here is the audit
protocol and evidence that the mechanism survives the first recognized
simulator bridge.

Two negative controls are important. First, easy classic-control settings are

testing whether the repair survives stronger domains. A rejection based on

evidence, or unfrozen test-time tuning should be much harder to sustain.

\subsection{Rebuttal Checklist}

If a reviewer argues that the paper is only synthetic, point to the v4
Gymnasium and MuJoCo bridge while agreeing that it is not a leaderboard-scale
robotics result. If a reviewer argues that pessimism is known, agree and
redirect to the selected-tail diagnostic and calibration-gated selection audit.
If a reviewer argues that posterior mean is competitive, agree and explain
that the claim targets posterior-sampled and upper-tail inference-time search.
If a reviewer argues that CEM is weak, agree that optimizing a flawed learned
model can fail and note that this is one of the paper's intended lessons. If a
reviewer argues that the MuJoCo intervals are wide, agree and keep the claim
comparative and scoped rather than statistical dominance.

The correct rebuttal posture is therefore not to oversell. The paper is
strongest when it is explicit: it identifies a real operator-level failure,
builds a reproducible battery of tests around that failure, reports both
positive and null results, and leaves large-scale policy learning as future
work.

\subsection{Future Benchmark Expansion}

The next evidence tier should preserve the same protocol while replacing the
compact bridge with larger accepted-paper benchmarks. The most direct upgrade
is online MPC in MuJoCo locomotion environments such as HalfCheetah, Hopper,
Walker2d, and Ant. That benchmark would test whether selected-tail failures
accumulate under repeated replanning and whether calibration-gated selection
can remain competitive when action dimensionality and horizon increase. The
claim gate should remain strict: report regret to a candidate oracle and
standard task return, but do not claim SOTA unless the protocol includes the
same training budgets and baselines as leaderboard papers.

A second upgrade is DMControl or Meta-World. These suites would test whether
the mechanism survives richer continuous-control tasks and sparse task
structure. The important design choice is to keep the selector audit separate
from policy-learning improvements. If a better representation or policy
optimizer improves every method, that is valuable, but it does not by itself
answer whether per-candidate posterior sampling over-selects epistemic tails.
The audit should therefore continue to compare mean, posterior sample,
Thompson-member, UCB, LCB, quantile, validation-gated, CEM, random, and
candidate oracle selection on identical starts and candidate pools.

A third upgrade is offline datasets such as D4RL or Minari. Here the risk is
especially relevant because support mismatch is explicit: candidate actions can
move outside the dataset distribution, and learned models can hallucinate
value. A D4RL-style version should report dataset support metrics, model
uncertainty, selected action OOD mass, and oracle regret under frozen offline
training data. It should also include conservative offline model-based
baselines so the paper does not merely rediscover known offline pessimism.

A fourth upgrade is visual latent world models. The selected-tail mechanism is
not limited to low-dimensional state. If a video or pixel-based world model
scores imagined futures with an ensemble or posterior sample, many-candidate
selection can still optimize an upper-tail hallucination. The challenge is
cost: visual world models require GPU training and careful asset management.
The v4 artifact intentionally keeps CPU reproducibility, but its diagnostics
can be ported directly to larger systems.

\subsection{Protocol Invariants for Future Work}

Future experiments should preserve the following invariants. First, final test
returns must be measured in the true environment or held-out benchmark target,
not in the learned model. Second, calibration data must be disjoint from final
test starts. Third, candidate pools must be shared across selectors whenever
the comparison is about selection rather than candidate generation. Fourth,
oracle regret must be reported against the same finite pool seen by deployed
selectors. Fifth, strong baselines such as CEM, scripted policies, or learned
policies must be labeled clearly as standalone baselines or as candidate-pool
augmentations. Sixth, protocol-freeze files should record seeds, tasks,
candidate budgets, metrics, baselines, thresholds, claim gates, and artifact
hashes before final reporting.

These invariants are more important than the particular ridge model used here.
Without them, the selected-tail claim can be made to look stronger or weaker by
accident. A weak candidate generator can hide selection failure because no
candidate is good. A candidate pool seeded with a strong teacher can hide
selection failure because every selector sees the teacher. A validation gate
tuned after test results can hide overfitting. The protocol is designed to make
these shortcuts visible.
